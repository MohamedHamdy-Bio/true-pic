Golgi Apparatus

COP-I beta1 (green) staining at the Golgi (GM130, a cis-Golgi marker) (red) in unstimulated HeLa cells. Helix Pomatia Lectin (HPL) (gray) staining is also shown.

Structure and Function

The Golgi apparatus, also known as the Golgi complex or Golgi body, is a membrane-bound organelle found in the cytoplasm of most eukaryotic cells. It consists of a series of flattened, stacked pouches called cisternae.

The Golgi apparatus receives proteins and lipids from the endoplasmic reticulum, modifies them, and packages them into vesicles for transport to their final destinations, such as lysosomes, the plasma membrane, or for secretion outside the cell.

Key Components

  • Cis face: The receiving side of the Golgi, facing the endoplasmic reticulum
  • Medial region: The middle processing compartments
  • Trans face: The shipping side of the Golgi, facing the plasma membrane
  • Transport vesicles: Small membrane-bound spheres that transport materials between organelles

Scientific Significance

The Golgi apparatus plays a crucial role in the processing and sorting of proteins and lipids. Dysfunction of the Golgi apparatus has been implicated in various diseases, including neurodegenerative disorders and certain types of cancer.

Mitochondria

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of mitochondria showing the characteristic cristae structures within the inner membrane.

Structure and Function

Mitochondria are membrane-bound organelles found in most eukaryotic cells. Often referred to as the "powerhouse of the cell," mitochondria generate most of the cell's supply of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the energy currency of cells.

Mitochondria have a distinctive structure with two membranes: an outer membrane and an inner membrane. The inner membrane is highly folded, forming structures called cristae, which increase the surface area for ATP-generating chemical reactions.

Key Components

  • Outer membrane: Permeable to small molecules and ions
  • Inner membrane: Contains proteins involved in electron transport and ATP synthesis
  • Cristae: Folds of the inner membrane that increase surface area
  • Matrix: The space within the inner membrane, containing enzymes, mitochondrial DNA, and ribosomes
  • Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA): Circular DNA that encodes some mitochondrial proteins

Scientific Significance

Beyond energy production, mitochondria are involved in various cellular processes, including calcium signaling, cell differentiation, and apoptosis (programmed cell death). Mitochondrial dysfunction has been linked to numerous diseases, including neurodegenerative disorders, diabetes, and aging.
